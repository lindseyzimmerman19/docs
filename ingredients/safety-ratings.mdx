---
title: "How the App's Ingredient Safety Rating Scale Works"
description: "Understand the 1–10 safety rating scale, what each level means, and the research and regulatory factors that determine every ingredient's score."
---

Every ingredient in the app's database carries a safety rating based on a synthesis of peer-reviewed dermatological research, regulatory assessments from bodies like the EU Scientific Committee on Consumer Safety and the US FDA, and published sensitization and irritation data. The goal is to give you a consistent, evidence-grounded baseline for each ingredient — not a marketing claim, but a summary of what the science says.

## The rating scale

Ratings run from 1 to 10 and fall into three bands. The color and number work together so you can read a result at a glance or look closer when you need detail.

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="Green / Safe (1–3)">
    Ingredients in this range are well-studied and widely tolerated across diverse skin types. They appear in a large body of clinical and observational research without significant signals for irritation, sensitization, or systemic concern. Examples include niacinamide, hyaluronic acid, and ceramides. A green rating does not mean the ingredient is inert — it means the evidence consistently supports a low risk profile for most people.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Yellow / Caution (4–6)">
    Ingredients in this range may cause reactions in some skin types or under certain conditions. The research picture is either mixed, limited, or shows meaningful sensitization rates in subpopulations — for example, people with rosacea, eczema, or existing contact allergies. Many commonly used ingredients fall here, including some preservatives, certain plant extracts, and mild surfactants. A yellow rating is a prompt to pay attention, not an automatic reason to avoid the ingredient.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Red / Concern (7–10)">
    Ingredients in this range are known irritants, documented allergens, or have been flagged by regulatory bodies or independent safety research for significant concerns across broad populations. Examples include high-concentration alcohol denaturants, formaldehyde-releasing preservatives, and certain synthetic fragrances. A red rating means the weight of evidence points to a meaningful risk for a large share of users.
  </Tab>
</Tabs>

## What factors determine a rating

No single data point sets an ingredient's score. The rating reflects a combination of factors evaluated across available research:

<CardGroup cols={2}>
  <Card title="Dermatological studies" icon="flask">
    Peer-reviewed clinical studies measuring irritation, sensitization, and tolerability, including patch test data and controlled exposure trials.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Regulatory status" icon="landmark">
    Whether the ingredient is restricted, banned, or subject to concentration limits under the EU Cosmetics Regulation, FDA guidelines, or other major regulatory frameworks.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Sensitization rates" icon="person">
    Published data on how often the ingredient triggers immune-mediated reactions in patch-tested populations, which reflects real-world allergy risk over time.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Comedogenic potential" icon="droplet">
    Research on how likely an ingredient is to clog pores, rated on a 0–5 comedogenicity scale and weighted alongside other safety factors.
  </Card>
</CardGroup>

## Ratings are population-level averages

The safety rating describes how an ingredient performs across a broad population — it is not a prediction of how your skin will respond. An ingredient rated "Caution" may be perfectly tolerated by your skin with no issues at all. Conversely, a "Safe" ingredient could still cause a reaction if you have a specific sensitivity that falls outside the documented norm.

Your personal skin profile shifts which concerns the app highlights for you, but the underlying numeric rating always reflects the population-level evidence base.

<Warning>
Safety ratings are informational summaries of published research and regulatory data. They are not medical advice and should not replace a consultation with a board-certified dermatologist, especially if you have a diagnosed skin condition, known allergies, or are pregnant.
</Warning>

<Tip>
Open your skin profile settings to tell the app about your skin type, known sensitivities, and concerns you want to prioritize. When your profile is complete, the app adjusts which flagged ingredients are surfaced most prominently in your results — so a rating of "Caution" for an ingredient you're specifically sensitive to will be highlighted more urgently than one that's irrelevant to your skin.
</Tip>
