---

Beyond the overall safety rating, the app tags ingredients with specific concern categories that give you more targeted information about why an ingredient may be relevant to your skin. A concern flag doesn't mean an ingredient is dangerous — it means there's a documented reason to be aware of it, and the details below explain what each category actually represents.

## Concern categories

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Fragrance / Parfum">
    Fragrance is one of the most common causes of contact dermatitis in skincare. On a product label, "fragrance" or "parfum" is a legally permitted umbrella term that can represent a blend of dozens — sometimes hundreds — of individual chemical compounds, both synthetic and naturally derived. Because the specific chemicals don't have to be disclosed, it's impossible to know exactly what you're being exposed to from the label alone. The app flags any ingredient declared as fragrance or parfum, and where individual fragrant compounds are listed separately (such as linalool or limonene), it flags those individually as well. Fragrance sensitivity can develop over time with repeated exposure, even if you've tolerated scented products in the past.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Potential Allergen">
    This category covers ingredients that are documented allergens — meaning they have the capacity to trigger an immune-mediated hypersensitivity reaction in susceptible individuals. The EU Cosmetics Regulation requires 26 specific fragrance allergens to be declared on labels above certain concentrations, but allergens extend beyond fragrance: certain preservatives, botanical extracts, and even some vitamins can act as sensitizers. Unlike irritation (which is a direct chemical effect), an allergic reaction involves your immune system and tends to worsen with repeated exposure. If you have a history of contact allergies, treat ingredients in this category with extra caution.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Comedogenic">
    Comedogenic ingredients have a higher likelihood of clogging pores, which can contribute to blackheads, whiteheads, and acne breakouts, particularly in people who are already acne-prone. The app references a 0–5 comedogenicity scale, where 0 means no pore-clogging potential and 5 means highly comedogenic. Ingredients rated 2 or below are generally considered safe even for acne-prone skin; ingredients rated 3 and above are flagged. Keep in mind that comedogenicity data comes from rabbit ear models and human patch studies, and real-world response depends heavily on formula concentration, product type, and individual skin physiology. An ingredient with a comedogenicity rating of 3 in a rinse-off cleanser poses far less risk than the same ingredient in a heavy leave-on cream.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Alcohol (Drying)">
    Not all alcohols behave the same way in skincare, and this flag applies specifically to short-chain or denatured alcohols — including ethanol, denatured alcohol (alcohol denat.), isopropyl alcohol, and SD alcohols. These types of alcohol evaporate quickly, which creates a fast-drying or matte effect, but at higher concentrations they can disrupt the skin's lipid barrier, strip away natural moisturizing factors, and increase transepidermal water loss over time. This is distinct from fatty alcohols such as cetyl alcohol, stearyl alcohol, and cetearyl alcohol, which are derived from natural fats and function as emollients and thickeners — those are not flagged under this category and are generally considered skin-friendly.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Endocrine Disruptor (Suspected)">
    Some ingredients used in skincare — particularly certain preservatives and UV filters — have been flagged in scientific literature for potential endocrine-disrupting activity, meaning they may interact with hormone signaling pathways in the body. Examples include benzophenone-3 (oxybenzone), some parabens at high concentrations, and certain synthetic musks. The evidence for endocrine disruption in humans from cosmetic exposure is still an active area of research, and regulatory bodies differ in their conclusions. The app uses "suspected" to reflect that these ingredients have been raised as a concern in peer-reviewed research but have not been confirmed as harmful at typical cosmetic use levels. People who are pregnant, breastfeeding, or have hormone-sensitive conditions may want to discuss these ingredients with a healthcare provider.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Sulfate / Harsh Surfactant">
    Surfactants are cleansing agents that attract both oil and water, allowing them to lift dirt and sebum off the skin. Sulfate-based surfactants — most commonly sodium lauryl sulfate (SLS) and sodium laureth sulfate (SLES) — are highly effective cleansers but are also among the most aggressive. At higher concentrations, they can disrupt the skin's acid mantle (the slightly acidic surface layer that supports barrier function and microbiome health), cause immediate irritation, and contribute to chronic dryness in people with sensitive or compromised skin. SLS tends to be more irritating than SLES because it has a smaller molecular size and penetrates deeper into the skin. Gentler alternatives include glucosides, amphoacetates, and betaines, which the app does not flag under this category.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Paraben Preservative">
    Parabens are a class of synthetic preservatives widely used in cosmetics and personal care products since the 1950s. They're effective at preventing bacterial and fungal growth and have a long safety record at approved concentrations. However, some studies have raised questions about their potential weak estrogenic activity, and parabens have been detected in human tissue samples, which prompted concern about cumulative exposure. The EU has restricted certain longer-chain parabens (propylparaben and butylparaben in leave-on products applied to the nappy area of children under three), and some brands have reformulated to avoid them entirely in response to consumer demand. The app flags parabens not because they're confirmed hazards at approved levels, but because many users actively choose to avoid them, and transparency about their presence is useful.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Essential Oil">
    Essential oils are concentrated aromatic extracts derived from plants, and they're frequently used in products marketed as "natural," "organic," or "clean." Despite their botanical origin, essential oils are among the most common causes of contact sensitization in skincare. Many contain naturally occurring fragrance allergens — linalool in lavender oil, eugenol in clove oil, limonene in citrus oils — that must be declared separately on EU-regulated labels when present above threshold concentrations. Sensitization to essential oils can develop gradually and may cross-react with other fragrance compounds. People with sensitive, reactive, or rosacea-prone skin are generally advised to treat products containing essential oils with the same caution as synthetic fragrance.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

<Note>
A flagged ingredient doesn't automatically make a product unsuitable for you. Context matters: the ingredient's concentration in the formula, whether the product is rinse-off or leave-on, how often you use it, and how your own skin responds are all factors that a flag alone can't capture. Use concern categories as a starting point for awareness, not as a definitive verdict.
</Note>
